A step-by-step playbook for Rosc to use Space Force relationships for deal flow, diligence, and portfolio support.
🎯 Step 1 — Map your portfolio to a funded requirement
Before any engagement, answer: which PEO has a funded line item matching what this company does? If there's no funded requirement, there's no contract. SpaceWERX SBIR/STRATFI is the exception — it can create new requirements. Use SpaceWERX topic solicitations, SDA RFPs, and DIU CSOs to map fit before investing.
🔑 Step 2 — Get into the SpaceWERX portfolio early
STRATFI explicitly requires VC co-investment — SpaceWERX needs VCs as partners. Arthur Grijalva has publicly said they are building investor relationships. A company with a STRATFI has government validation of technology — the best diligence signal available. PY26.1 STRATFI deadline May 29, 2026.
💰 Step 3 — Use STRATFI as a co-investment signal
When Space Force issues a STRATFI, it puts government money in alongside VC money. K2 Space ($60M STRATFI), Varda ($60M STRATFI), True Anomaly (SpaceWERX early engagement → $1B+ raised) all used this model. Track awards at spacewerx.us/news for deal flow signals.
🤝 Step 4 — Build DIU relationships before you need them
DIU is the most VC-friendly DoD organization. Explicit investor relations programming. Mountain View office is the primary contact. DIU OnRamp Hubs host office hours at Capital Factory (Austin) and other innovation hubs. Show up — these are genuine relationship-building events, not bureaucratic formalities.
📡 Step 5 — Monitor SDA solicitations for portfolio pipeline
SDA publishes RFPs publicly at sda.mil/opportunities. Each PWSA tranche is a separate competition. Winning an SDA tranche is a strong signal for a Series A/B — real customer, real revenue, 2–3 year delivery contract. Most realistic path to government revenue for a hardware startup in the space domain.
🚫 Step 6 — Know what Space Force will NOT commercialize
Space Force explicitly will NOT seek commercial solutions for: missile warning, combat power projection, electromagnetic warfare, and nuclear detonation detection. Commercial sweet spots: SATCOM, SDA, PNT, launch, C2 software, ISR data processing, cyber, and ground systems.

Each Program Executive Office (PEO) inside Space Systems Command manages a specific portfolio of programs. This is where contracts actually originate. Understanding each PEO tells you which bucket your portfolio company belongs in — and what kind of contract to expect.
How PEOs work: Each PEO is led by a military officer (usually a colonel or general) and a civilian program executive. They manage a budget, run competitions, and award contracts — typically FAR-based for large programs, OTA for rapid/prototype work, and SBIR/STRATFI through SpaceWERX for commercial innovation. Deal sizes range from $1M SBIR Phase IIs to multi-billion IDIQ vehicles that span decades.
PEO Space C2 — Space Command & Control
What it owns
All software systems that command and control Space Force satellites and operations. Space C2 (SpC2) modernization program. Battle management for Golden Dome. GPS OCX ground system. JADC2 integration for Space Force nodes.
Budget signal
~$800M annually. OCX alone has consumed $1B+. SpC2 modernization is a multi-year, multi-hundred-million dollar effort. Golden Dome BM software is emerging as a new major line item.
Contract vehicles used
FAR-based IDIQ (large programs like OCX). OTA for prototypes (SpC2 increments). SBIR/STRATFI for commercial software startups through SpaceWERX.
Current activity (2026)
SpC2 incremental development ongoing. Golden Dome battle management software emerging as new requirement. Active push away from monolithic ground software toward modular cloud-native architecture.
Why it matters for VC
The most software-heavy PEO. Every lesson from OCX (single vendor, waterfall, years late) is driving a push toward commercial software approaches. Cloud-native multi-mission ground software and autonomous constellation management are both active unsolved problems this PEO needs to solve. Palantir's Space Force relationships run through here.
PEO GPS & Navigation Systems (NSS)
What it owns
GPS III and GPS IIIF satellites (Lockheed Martin). GPS OCX ground control system (Raytheon). Military GPS User Equipment — MGUE Inc 1 and Inc 2 (L3Harris). Enhanced Polar System Recapitalization (EPS-R).
Budget signal
~$1.2B annually. GPS III follow-on added 2 satellites in FY25 ($260M+). OCX has been $1B+ over many years. MGUE procurement is ongoing across the services.
Contract vehicles used
Large FAR-based production contracts (GPS III with Lockheed Martin, OCX with Raytheon). Separate competitive buys for user equipment. Some OTA for alternative PNT experimentation.
Current activity (2026)
GPS IIIF production ongoing. OCX testing — 98% test pass rate as of April 2025 but still troubled. MGUE Inc 2 procurement accelerating. Growing interest in commercial alternative PNT (LEO-PNT) as GPS jamming threats increase.
Why it matters for VC
The most conservative PEO — dominated by two primes (Lockheed, Raytheon) and slow procurement cycles. The VC angle is in the commercial adjacent space: anti-jam/anti-spoof chipsets for MGUE alternatives, and LEO-PNT constellations that provide GPS backup. Xona Space is the main LEO-PNT player. A GPS jamming event in a major conflict would accelerate this category overnight.
PEO MILSATCOM — Military Satellite Communications
What it owns
AEHF (nuclear command SATCOM, 6 satellites). WGS (wideband SATCOM backbone, 11 satellites). Protected Tactical Services (PTS, new jam-resistant system). Commercial Augmentation Space Reserve (CASR) for SATCOM. SDA transport layer coordination.
Budget signal
~$3.7B annually — the largest single mission area in Space Force. AEHF and WGS are operational (sustainment costs). PTS is an active new development. CASR SATCOM expansion is funded for 2026.
Contract vehicles used
Large FAR IDIQ for legacy programs (AEHF sustainment, WGS). OTA and CASR agreements for commercial SATCOM. SpaceWERX SBIR for commercial integration tools. The CASR framework is specifically designed as a non-FAR commercial vehicle.
Current activity (2026)
CASR expanding from SDA pilot to SATCOM — new vendor onboarding underway. PTS development active. Commercial SATCOM wargames completed with 14+ companies in 2025. Next big competition: Protected Tactical SATCOM follow-on.
Why it matters for VC
The most commercially open PEO. CASR is a genuine on-ramp — pre-negotiated agreements with commercial SATCOM providers that can be activated during a crisis. New SATCOM vendors are being onboarded now. The interoperability software gap (switching between Starlink, SES, and legacy MILSATCOM) is a real unsolved problem this PEO needs. Most commercial space VC deal flow touching SATCOM eventually runs through this office.
PEO Launch Enterprise
What it owns
National Security Space Launch (NSSL) program — all Space Force launch contracts. Range operations (Cape Canaveral, Vandenberg). Rocket Cargo program. Tactically Responsive Space (TacRS) launch. Small sat launch (NSSL Lane 1).
Budget signal
~$2B+ annually. NSSL Phase 3 has two lane holders: SpaceX (Falcon 9/Heavy) and ULA (Vulcan Centaur). Lane 1 (medium/small launch) adds Rocket Lab and others. Maj. Gen. Purdy has explicitly said they are "satisfied with launch" — new launch companies are not a priority investment area.
Contract vehicles used
Large FAR IDIQ for NSSL (multi-year, multi-billion). OTA for TacRS demonstrations. SBIR/STRATFI for responsive launch technology (SpaceWERX issued only 1 STRATFI to a launch company — ABL Space, which exited the market).
Current activity (2026)
NSSL Phase 3 active with SpaceX and ULA. Lane 1 expanding. TacRS demonstrations continuing (VICTUS HAZE follow-ons). Range modernization ongoing. Purdy's Feb 2026 investor talk explicitly said payloads and sensors are the gap — not launch vehicles.
Why it matters for VC
The most closed PEO for new entrants. SpaceX effectively owns this market. The VC opportunity is not in launch vehicles — it's in launch-adjacent software: range safety automation, payload integration tooling, responsive launch operations software (scheduling 24-hour launch windows). These are software problems, not rocket problems, and they're currently solved manually.
PEO Space Combat Power — Col. Bryon McClain
What it owns
Space-Based Interceptors (SBIs) for Golden Dome. Offensive and defensive counterspace capabilities (mostly classified). Space electronic warfare. Kill chain software for space-based weapons. On-orbit maneuvering and rendezvous programs.
Budget signal
$3.2B already awarded in OTA prototype contracts (late 2025/early 2026). $25B allocated to Golden Dome over 10 years by Congress. Single largest new program in Space Force history. Most of the $13.8B reconciliation surge flows through or adjacent to this PEO.
Contract vehicles used
Predominantly OTA — the 20 SBI contracts were all OTA. This is intentional: OTA "attracted both traditional and non-traditional vendors" per Col. McClain. Competitive parallel development (multiple companies building competing prototypes simultaneously) rather than selecting a single winner early.
Current activity (2026)
SBI prototype development underway across 12 firms. Initial capability target: 2028. Next phase competitions expected 2026-2027 as prototypes are evaluated. Kill chain software (battle management) is the identified gap with no current incumbent. Much of the work is classified.
Why it matters for VC
The hottest PEO for defense-tech VC right now. True Anomaly ($650M raised after SBI selection) is the clearest example of what a win here does to a company's trajectory. The hardware (interceptors) is now covered by 12 companies. The software — kill chain management, autonomous targeting, battle damage assessment, constellation coordination — has no incumbent. This is the biggest unsolved software problem in space defense. Companies need security clearances and ITAR compliance to play here, which raises the bar but also narrows the competition.
PEO Intelligence, Surveillance & Reconnaissance (ISR) & Novel Capabilities
What it owns
Overhead Persistent Infrared (OPIR / Next-Gen OPIR) missile warning satellites. Wide Field of View (WFOV) sensors. Hypersonic and Ballistic Tracking Space Sensor (HBTSS). Commercial ISR data purchases. Novel sensing programs (many classified).
Budget signal
~$900M annually for unclassified programs. Classified budget adds significantly to this. Next-Gen OPIR is a multi-billion satellite program. HBTSS is an active development. Commercial ISR data purchases are growing as an alternative to building government sensors.
Contract vehicles used
Large FAR IDIQ for major satellite programs (Next-Gen OPIR with Northrop/Lockheed). OTA and SBIR for novel sensing. Commercial data purchase agreements (Other Transactions) for buying imagery and tracking data from commercial providers.
Current activity (2026)
HBTSS development ongoing. Commercial data buying expanding — Space Force increasingly purchasing ISR data from commercial providers rather than building dedicated government sensors. On-orbit AI inference is an emerging requirement: processing data on the satellite before downlink to reduce latency.
Why it matters for VC
The commercial data purchase trend is significant — it means startups with geospatial AI and hyperspectral sensing capabilities can sell data products to the Space Force without winning a traditional satellite contract. HawkEye 360 (RF intelligence) and Slingshot Aerospace (space tracking) are examples. On-orbit AI inference (edge compute on satellites) is an emerging requirement with no current commercial incumbent that meets Space Force standards.
SDA — Space Development Agency (separate from PEO structure but functions like one)
What it owns
PWSA constellation — Transport Layer (data relay), Tracking Layer (missile tracking), and future layers. 300+ satellites across multiple tranches. Optical inter-satellite links. Ground architecture for PWSA. Airborne optical comms terminals (new RFI 2025).
Budget signal
Multi-billion over 5+ years. Tranche 1 (28 transport + 20 tracking satellites) already launched Sept 2025. T3TRK (54+ tracking satellites) is the current active solicitation. SDA moves faster than any other Space Force acquisition organization.
Contract vehicles used
Predominantly OTA — SDA pioneered the use of OTA for satellite acquisition. Multiple simultaneous competitors per tranche. 2-3 year delivery requirement built into every contract. Also participates in SpaceWERX SBIR for component technologies.
Current activity (2026)
T3TRK RFP active (54+ tracking satellites, proposals due). Airborne optical communications terminal RFI issued. Transport Layer Tranche 2 under construction. First on-orbit demonstrations of space-to-air laser comms completed Aug 2025. Planning for Tranche 3 and beyond.
Why it matters for VC
The single best government customer signal for hardware startups. SDA contract = real revenue, real timeline (2-3 years), real validation. Winners of SDA tranches (York Space, Teledyne, Rocket Lab, L3Harris) have used it as a Series B/C anchor. The next opportunity: T3TRK tracking layer satellites. Also watch: airborne optical comms terminals — a new market connecting aircraft directly to LEO satellites via laser, with both military and commercial applications.

The basics — what is the Space Force?
USSF — United States Space Force
The sixth and newest branch of the US military, created by Congress in December 2019. Think of it like the Army or Navy, but focused entirely on space. Its job is to protect US interests in space (satellites, GPS, missile warning systems) and to make sure adversaries like China and Russia can't take those away. There are about 19,000 people ("Guardians") in the Space Force. It operates within the Department of the Air Force alongside the US Air Force — similar to how the Marine Corps sits inside the Department of the Navy.
CSO — Chief of Space Operations
The most senior military officer in the Space Force, equivalent to the Army's Chief of Staff or the Navy's Chief of Naval Operations. Currently General B. Chance Saltzman. He sets the overall strategy and direction for the entire Space Force and reports to the Secretary of Defense. Think of him as the CEO of the Space Force.
DoD — Department of Defense
The entire US military establishment — Army, Navy, Air Force, Marines, Space Force, Coast Guard, and all the civilian and contractor support around them. The Secretary of Defense (currently Pete Hegseth) runs it and reports to the President. The DoD's annual budget is roughly $900 billion.
DAF — Department of the Air Force
The cabinet-level department that houses both the US Air Force and the US Space Force. Led by the Secretary of the Air Force (currently Troy Meink). Think of it as the parent organization — the Space Force is one of two services inside it.
The command structure
SSC — Space Systems Command
The Space Force organization responsible for buying and building things — satellites, software, ground systems, launch vehicles. If you want to sell something to the Space Force, SSC is almost always the door you knock on. $11 billion annual budget. Headquartered in El Segundo, California (near Los Angeles). Led by a three-star general (Lt. Gen. Philip Garrant). Equivalent of a giant defense procurement office.
SpOC — Space Operations Command
The Space Force organization that actually operates the satellites and systems SSC builds. They run the day-to-day — pointing satellites, commanding constellations, providing GPS and missile warning data to the military. They're the users; SSC is the buyer. Headquartered at Peterson Space Force Base in Colorado Springs.
STARCOM — Space Training and Readiness Command
Trains Space Force Guardians and develops doctrine (the official playbook for how to do things in space operations). Least relevant to VC deal flow unless you're building training simulators or war-gaming software.
SDA — Space Development Agency
A relatively new organization inside the Space Force specifically designed to buy satellites faster than the traditional process allows. Traditional DoD satellite programs take 10-15 years. SDA targets 2-3 year delivery. It's building the PWSA (see below) — a large constellation of small satellites in low Earth orbit. For startups, SDA contracts are the most realistic path to a government customer quickly.
PEO — Program Executive Office
Think of a PEO as a specialized procurement department within SSC. Each PEO owns a specific set of programs. PEO GPS manages GPS satellites. PEO MILSATCOM manages military communications satellites. PEO Space Combat Power manages the Golden Dome space weapons programs. If you want to sell to a specific program area, you eventually need to find the right PEO. There are roughly 8-10 PEOs under SSC.
DIU — Defense Innovation Unit
A Pentagon organization specifically created to bridge the gap between Silicon Valley commercial tech companies and the US military. Founded in 2015 with offices in Mountain View, CA (Silicon Valley), Austin, TX, and Boston. Unlike traditional DoD contracting, DIU uses a faster contract type (OTA) and actively recruits commercial companies that have never sold to the government before. Explicitly VC-friendly — they have investor relations staff and attend tech conferences. If your portfolio company wants to sell to DoD without going through a 3-year procurement process, DIU is the first call.
Acquisition & contracting
SBIR / STTR — Small Business Innovation Research / Small Business Tech Transfer
Government-funded R&D grants specifically for small businesses and startups. Phase I is $50K–$250K to prove a concept. Phase II is $750K–$1.5M to build a prototype. Phase III is when you commercialize — no funding cap, can lead to sole-source contracts. Think of Phase I/II as the government paying a startup to de-risk technology before buying it at scale. SBIR/STTR authorization lapsed in October 2025 but is expected to be reauthorized.
STRATFI — Strategic Funding Increase
A Space Force program that layers additional government funding ($3M–$15M) on top of a company's existing Phase II SBIR contract — but only if private investors co-invest matching funds. This is the most VC-friendly program in the Space Force. The government is saying: "We believe in this technology enough to put money in alongside yours." True Anomaly, Varda, and K2 Space all used STRATFI to scale. Managed by SpaceWERX. PY26.1 deadline is May 29, 2026.
TACFI — Tactical Funding Increase
The smaller version of STRATFI — $375K–$2M. Same concept (matching VC funds + government funds) but for earlier-stage or lower-priority technology. Good stepping stone toward a full STRATFI.
OTA — Other Transaction Authority
A special contracting authority that lets the government bypass the standard Federal Acquisition Regulations (FAR) procurement rules. Standard DoD procurement is notoriously slow and bureaucratic — OTA was created to speed it up, especially for prototype and research work. DIU uses OTA almost exclusively. The Golden Dome SBI contracts ($3.2B to 12 firms) were all OTA. Think of OTA as the government's "fast lane" for deals with commercial companies.
FAR — Federal Acquisition Regulations
The standard rulebook for how the US government buys things. Extremely detailed, slow, and bureaucratic. Written primarily for large defense contractors, not startups. When people say DoD contracting is hard, they mostly mean FAR-based contracting. OTA, SBIR, and DIU's CSOs were all created specifically to avoid FAR for commercial tech.
CSO — Commercial Solutions Opening
DIU's primary contract solicitation method. Unlike traditional RFPs that are written around a specific technical specification, CSOs describe a problem the government is trying to solve and invite any commercial company to propose a solution. Rolling admissions — you can submit anytime, not just during a specific window. No SBIR Phase II required. The most accessible DoD contract mechanism for a VC-backed startup.
TRL — Technology Readiness Level
A 1–9 scale the government uses to assess how mature a technology is. TRL 1 is basic research (it exists in a lab). TRL 9 is fully operational in the field. OTA contracts typically require TRL 6+ (prototype demonstrated in relevant environment). SBIR starts at TRL 1-3. STRATFI is typically TRL 4-6. This matters for VCs because it tells you how much technical de-risking still needs to happen before a government contract is realistic.
PPBE — Planning, Programming, Budgeting & Execution
The annual cycle the DoD uses to allocate money. Budget requests are submitted about 2 years before the money is actually available to spend. This is why selling to the government is slow — even if a program office loves your technology, there may be no money allocated for it until the next budget cycle. SBIR and OTA are partly designed to work around this by using pre-allocated innovation funds.
The innovation offices
SpaceWERX
The Space Force's dedicated innovation office, embedded within SSC. Manages the Space Force's SBIR/STTR portfolio ($470M budget), runs the STRATFI and TACFI programs, and organizes cohort programs that connect startups with Space Force program offices. Think of SpaceWERX as the Space Force's venture arm — they identify promising commercial technologies and help them find a path to government contract. Director: Arthur Grijalva. Website: spacewerx.us
AFRL — Air Force Research Laboratory
The DoD's primary space research organization. Based at Kirtland Air Force Base in New Mexico. Conducts basic and applied research for space technologies at early TRL levels (1-5). AFRL's Space Vehicles Directorate is the most relevant division. AFRL often co-invests in STRATFI contracts alongside SpaceWERX — providing government matching funds. Also provides test facilities (like the Starfire Optical Range for satellite tracking) that startups can access to validate technology.
AFWERX
The Air Force version of SpaceWERX — the Air Force's innovation arm. Runs SBIR/STTR programs for Air Force (not Space Force) technology needs. SpaceWERX was spun out of AFWERX specifically to focus on space. They still share some infrastructure and programs.
The satellites & systems
SATCOM — Satellite Communications
Using satellites to transmit voice, data, and video over long distances. The military relies on SATCOM for everything from drone control to strategic communications between commanders. MILSATCOM specifically means military-owned satellite communications (as opposed to commercial SATCOM like Starlink). The Space Force is the #1 user of satellite communications in the world.
MILSATCOM — Military Satellite Communications
Government-owned and operated military communications satellites. The main programs are AEHF (ultra-secure communications for nuclear command and control), WGS (Wideband Global SATCOM — the "internet backbone" for military), and the newer Protected Tactical Services (PTS, jam-resistant). Complements commercial SATCOM like Starlink/Starshield which the military leases during crises.
AEHF — Advanced Extremely High Frequency
A constellation of 6 highly encrypted communications satellites specifically designed to survive nuclear war and jamming. Used for nuclear command and control — the communications link between the President, nuclear submarines, bombers, and missile silos. Extremely hardened against electronic attack. Successor to the Milstar system.
WGS — Wideband Global SATCOM
The Space Force's primary high-bandwidth communications satellite system — basically the military's satellite internet backbone. 11 satellites in geosynchronous orbit. Used for everything from drone video feeds in Afghanistan to ship-to-shore communications. Less hardened than AEHF but much higher bandwidth.
PTS — Protected Tactical Services / SATCOM
A newer jam-resistant military satellite communications program. Designed to work even when adversaries are actively trying to jam or spoof the signals. Replaces older protected SATCOM systems. Uses advanced anti-jam waveforms and frequency-hopping techniques.
GPS — Global Positioning System
The US military's satellite navigation system — the same one in your phone, but originally built for and still operated by the Space Force. 31 operational satellites in medium Earth orbit. Every US military system from guided missiles to soldier wearables relies on GPS for timing and positioning. GPS jamming and spoofing (making GPS show the wrong location) are serious active threats — Russia and Iran both do it routinely in conflict zones.
GPS OCX — GPS Operational Control System (Next Generation)
The ground software system that controls GPS III satellites. A notoriously troubled program — over $1 billion spent, years behind schedule, still not fully operational. Managed by Raytheon for the PEO GPS office. A cautionary tale about what happens when complex C2 software is built the old way (massive contracts, single vendor, waterfall development).
MGUE — Military GPS User Equipment
The military GPS receivers used by soldiers, aircraft, ships, and missiles. More jam-resistant and secure than civilian GPS. Two generations are currently being procured: Inc 1 (baseline anti-jam capability) and Inc 2 (more advanced). Getting MGUE into the hands of every military user is a multi-year procurement effort.
OPIR — Overhead Persistent Infrared
A class of satellites that detect missile launches by the infrared (heat) signature of rocket engines. The Space Force's primary early warning system for ballistic missiles and increasingly hypersonic weapons. Current generation is the SBIRS system. Next generation (Next-Gen OPIR) is being developed by Northrop Grumman and Lockheed Martin.
PWSA — Proliferated Warfighter Space Architecture
The Space Development Agency's plan to build a large constellation of small satellites in low Earth orbit (LEO) that provide communications, missile tracking, and data transport to military users. "Proliferated" means many small cheap satellites instead of a few big expensive ones — harder to knock out. Target is 300+ satellites. Think of it as the Space Force's version of Starlink but for classified military use.
HBTSS — Hypersonic and Ballistic Tracking Space Sensor
A program to put sensors in low Earth orbit that can track hypersonic missiles — the fast, maneuvering weapons that China and Russia are developing specifically to evade traditional missile defense. Hypersonic missiles are hard to track because they don't follow predictable ballistic trajectories. HBTSS provides the "eyes" for systems like THAAD and the future Golden Dome.
WFOV — Wide Field of View
A type of space sensor that covers a large area of Earth at once — sacrificing detail for coverage. Complements narrow field of view (NFOV) sensors that see less area but in higher resolution. Used for missile launch detection and space domain awareness where you need to watch a lot of sky simultaneously.
EPS-R — Enhanced Polar System Recapitalization
Replacement satellites for the Enhanced Polar System — a protected communications capability specifically for military users in the Arctic and polar regions, where GPS and standard SATCOM don't work well. The replacement payloads were hosted on commercial Norwegian satellites (Space Norway) and launched in August 2024, saving about $900M vs building dedicated government satellites.
Orbits, physics & the space environment
LEO — Low Earth Orbit
The region of space from about 200 to 2,000 km altitude. Satellites here move fast (circle Earth every 90 minutes) and are close enough that signals travel with low latency — good for communications and sensing. SpaceX's Starlink operates in LEO. SDA's PWSA constellation is being built in LEO. The trade-off: individual LEO satellites see a small area at any moment, so you need many of them for global coverage.
MEO — Medium Earth Orbit
The region from about 2,000 to 35,000 km altitude. GPS satellites orbit here at about 20,200 km. MEO provides global coverage with fewer satellites than LEO but introduces more signal delay (latency). The Van Allen radiation belts also pass through MEO, making electronics harder to protect.
GEO — Geostationary Orbit
A specific orbit at 35,786 km altitude where a satellite's orbital speed matches Earth's rotation — it appears to hover in the same spot in the sky. Traditional communications satellites (like DirecTV) and weather satellites use GEO. Advantage: one satellite covers a third of Earth continuously. Disadvantage: signals take 240+ ms to travel up and back (noticeable lag), and satellites are expensive single points of failure.
RF — Radio Frequency
The electromagnetic spectrum used for wireless communications — radio, radar, satellite uplinks, GPS signals. "RF intelligence" means detecting and analyzing radio signals from other satellites or ground systems. Adversary satellites emit RF signatures that can be detected passively (without active radar) — an important source of space domain awareness information.
Missile defense & weapons
SBI — Space-Based Interceptor
A satellite armed with a kill vehicle (essentially a guided projectile) that can destroy enemy ballistic missiles shortly after launch — the "boost phase" when the rocket engine is still burning and the missile is moving slowly and predictably. The key component of the Golden Dome space layer. 20 prototype contracts ($3.2B total) awarded to 12 companies in late 2025/early 2026. Initial capability targeted for 2028.
Golden Dome
President Trump's program (announced May 2025) to build a layered missile defense system protecting the entire US homeland — inspired by Israel's Iron Dome but vastly more ambitious. The "golden" refers to the space layer (SBIs in orbit). Total estimated cost: $175B+ over 10-20 years. FY2026 got $13.8B via reconciliation. This is the single largest new Space Force program and is driving most of the current VC excitement in the space defense sector.
CASR — Commercial Augmentation Space Reserve
A program modeled on the Air Force's Civil Reserve Air Fleet (which lets the military call on commercial airlines during a crisis). CASR pre-negotiates agreements with commercial space companies so the Space Force can "surge" their capabilities during a conflict — more communications bandwidth, more satellite imagery, more tracking data. Currently in pilot phase for SDA (4 companies, started March 2025). Expanding to SATCOM in 2026. This is a genuine on-ramp for commercial space startups to get paid government contracts.
NSSL — National Security Space Launch
The Space Force's primary launch procurement program. Buys launch services from commercial providers to put military satellites into orbit. Currently two contract holders: SpaceX (Falcon 9 and Falcon Heavy) and ULA (Vulcan Centaur). There's also a smaller "Lane 1" for medium/small satellites that includes Rocket Lab and others. NSSL contracts are worth billions over multi-year periods and are the lifeblood of the launch industry.
TacRS — Tactically Responsive Space
The ability to launch a satellite within hours or days of a need being identified — not the current reality of months-to-years planning cycles. The Space Force wants this capability to quickly replace satellites that are damaged or destroyed in conflict. VICTUS HAZE (2024) demonstrated this with True Anomaly and Rocket Lab. SpaceWERX runs a TacRS challenge program.
Intelligence & surveillance
ISR — Intelligence, Surveillance & Reconnaissance
The collection of information about adversaries using sensors. "Intelligence" is the analysis. "Surveillance" is continuous monitoring of an area or target. "Reconnaissance" is targeted observation of a specific location or asset. From space, ISR includes satellite imagery, electronic signal collection, missile tracking, and ship monitoring. The Space Force provides ISR data to every branch of the military and to intelligence agencies like the CIA and NSA.
SDA (as a concept) — Space Domain Awareness
Knowing what's in space: where every satellite is, what it's doing, who owns it, and whether it's a threat. The US currently tracks over 50,000 objects in orbit. As China and Russia put more satellites up — including satellites designed to maneuver close to US satellites and potentially disable them — SDA becomes a critical national security function. Companies like LeoLabs and True Anomaly provide commercial SDA data.
Command, control & software
C2 — Command and Control
The systems and processes used by military commanders to direct forces and operations. In space, C2 means the software and hardware used to command satellites — telling them where to point, what data to collect, when to transmit. The Space Force's Space C2 (SpC2) modernization program is trying to build software that can command thousands of satellites simultaneously. Current systems were built when the Space Force had dozens of satellites; PWSA will have hundreds.
BM — Battle Management
The systems that help military commanders make decisions during a battle — fusing sensor data, tracking threats, coordinating responses, and recommending actions. In the Golden Dome context, BM is the software that takes data from thousands of tracking satellites, identifies incoming missiles, and directs interceptors to the right place at the right time. No single company currently provides this for space-based missile defense.
JADC2 — Joint All-Domain Command and Control
A DoD-wide initiative to connect all military sensors and shooters across land, sea, air, space, and cyber into a single networked system. The idea: a soldier in a desert can see data from a satellite and direct a Navy ship to respond, all automatically. Space Force's space systems are a key node in this network. Still largely in development — one of the most ambitious and most troubled programs in DoD.
VC & investment terms in this context
Dual-use
Technology that has both commercial and military applications. Starlink is dual-use — originally built for consumer internet, now also used by the US military (as Starshield). Vector databases (like Pinecone) are dual-use — originally built for commercial AI, now relevant for defense AI. Dual-use companies are generally more attractive to VCs because they don't depend on the government as their only customer.
Valley of Death
The gap between a company winning a research contract (Phase II SBIR) and getting a large production contract. The government funds early R&D and eventually buys mature systems at scale — but there's a painful middle period where neither phase is funded. Many defense startups die here. STRATFI was specifically created to help companies cross this gap. The "second valley of death" in the space context refers to the gap between designing a satellite and being able to manufacture it at scale.
OTA (in VC context)
Other Transaction Authority — not to be confused with Over The Air (software updates). In defense, OTA is the fast-lane contract type. For VCs, a portfolio company with an OTA contract has proven it can work with DoD without the 3-year procurement nightmare. OTA contracts are also a signal that the program office sees this as genuinely innovative technology, not a commodity purchase.
Prime contractor / "the primes"
The large, established defense companies that have historically dominated DoD contracts: Lockheed Martin, Northrop Grumman, Raytheon (now RTX), Boeing, General Dynamics, L3Harris. They win the major contracts and typically subcontract work to smaller companies. The Space Force's commercial integration push is explicitly designed to reduce dependence on the primes and bring in non-traditional (VC-backed) companies. When this document says a winner is a "prime," it means one of these legacy giants.
